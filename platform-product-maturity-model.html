Platform Product Maturity Model

The platform's purpose is to maximise the time developers spend on product work by giving them the context to make decisions about their systems that matter, while abstracting the complexity of getting that information and managing those systems.

Self-ServiceCan developers accomplish what they need without waiting for someone else?
Fleet ManagementCan the organisation understand, govern, and act on its services as a whole?
Company-SpecificDoes the platform encode your organisation's conventions, constraints, and context?

Level 1 Ad Hoc Tribal knowledge and tickets
Disconnected planning. Ideas translated to work manually. Planning is a separate activity. Ideas are captured in disconnected tools and translated manually into technical work. The output of a planning session — a ticket, a doc, a sketch — has no automated relationship to the systems that will implement it.
Bespoke infra and patterns per team. No shared rationale. Unpredictable costs. Each team makes bespoke choices about infrastructure, dependencies, and application patterns with no shared rationale. Distributed system concerns — retries, delivery guarantees, resilience — are solved independently per team, or not at all. Costs are varied and unpredictable.
Each team's own process. Varies by service and who set it up. Each team has their own way of getting work to production — whether that's a backend deploy, a frontend build, or a mobile release. The process varies by team and by who set it up. Quality gates are inconsistent or absent.
Disconnected tools. No single view of services, health, or cost. Knowing what's happening means checking multiple disconnected tools — if they exist. No single view of services, ownership, health, dependencies, or cost.
Each team's problem. No shared approach. Response depends on who notices. Keeping things healthy is each team's problem. There's no shared approach to scaling, resource management, or routine maintenance. When something needs attention, the response depends on who notices first.
Finding logs takes longer than the fix. No path from alert to diagnosis. When something breaks, finding the relevant logs and traces takes longer than the fix. There's no consistent path from alert to diagnosis.
Ad hoc projects. No fleet view. No way to assess impact. Migrations, upgrades, and decommissions are ad hoc projects. There's no fleet-wide view and no easy way to assess impact.
Level 2 Standardised Shared defaults, direct tooling
Planning tools integrated with dev tooling. Work items flow without re-entry. Planning tools are integrated with developer tooling, enabling a smoother transition between product thinking and technical execution. Work items flow from planning into development without manual re-entry.
Templates and shared libraries for common patterns. Teams adopt independently. Templates and guidelines exist for common infrastructure designs and application patterns. Common ingress, egress, and backing service patterns are maintained centrally. Shared libraries cover concerns like retries and circuit breakers. Teams adopt these but integrate and maintain them independently. Costs become more predictable where standards are followed.
Shared standards for build/test/release. Teams own their config. Shared standards define expected stages for build, test, and release across disciplines. Teams adopt them but own their configuration. Mobile, web, and backend may have separate standards but they exist and are documented.
Catalogue and baseline observability exist. Manual upkeep, tends to drift. A catalogue tracks services and ownership. Observability has a baseline — standard metrics, logging, and alerting. But the catalogue relies on teams keeping their information up to date manually, so it has a tendency to fall out of sync with reality.
Sensible defaults in place. Tuning, patching, and scaling still on developers. Sensible defaults are in place so the most common operational needs are covered out of the box. Developers inherit good starting points, but acting on issues — tuning, patching, scaling — is still on them.
Logs and metrics findable. Connecting the dots still manual. Consistent observability means logs and metrics are findable. The data is there, but connecting the dots during an incident — correlating a deploy with a spike, tracing a failure across services — is still manual.
Consistent rollouts possible. Coordinating fleet-wide changes still effortful. Shared foundations mean changes can be rolled out consistently. But coordinating a fleet-wide change is still effortful — the building blocks exist but the workflow doesn't.
Level 3 Self-Service Paths Declare intent, platform handles it
Design systems integrated from the start. Company guidelines enforced at ideation. Design systems are integrated with planning and developer tooling. New work uses existing components, patterns, and interfaces from the start — enforcing company guidelines and visual consistency from the point of ideation, not as a review gate later.
Declare intent, platform provisions infra and handles complexity. Predictable costs. Infrastructure and application patterns are embedded in self-service offerings. Developers declare intent — "this service needs a document database" or "this service publishes order events" — and the platform provisions the infrastructure and handles the complexity: outbox patterns, dead letter handling, schema compatibility. Designs are standardised with predictable costs.
Platform provides and maintains pipelines. Push code, platform handles the rest. Pipelines are provided and maintained by the platform, appropriate to the type of work — backend service, web application, mobile release. Developers push code; the platform handles the rest. Quality, security, and compliance gates are part of the process, not a separate activity.
Catalogue accurate by construction. Observability automatic. One place for everything. The catalogue is accurate by construction. Observability is automatic and comprehensive — health, dependencies, cost, and compliance state are visible without configuring anything. Developers see what matters in one place.
Routine ops handled by platform. Developers act only on decisions. Routine operational actions are handled by the platform. Certificate rotation, dependency patching, base image updates, scaling within declared parameters — these happen without developer intervention. Developers act when the platform surfaces something that requires a decision.
Platform surfaces context in one place. Developers diagnose, not search. The platform surfaces relevant context: recent changes, related services, logs, and known vulnerabilities — all in one place. Developers spend time diagnosing, not searching.
Paths for common ops. Fleet-wide and novel changes still bespoke. The platform provides paths for common lifecycle operations — dependency updates, environment promotion, decommissioning. But novel or cross-cutting fleet-wide changes still require bespoke effort.
Level 4 Integrated Product SaaS-like, full lifecycle
Natural language to prototypes and work item breakdowns using existing patterns. Non-engineers can describe ideas in natural language and have them translated into high-fidelity prototypes using existing interfaces, design systems, and service patterns. The platform generates a breakdown of work items with appropriate specification and detail, ready for development.
Infra and patterns managed as a connected system. Evolved centrally. The platform manages infrastructure and application patterns as a connected system. Service dependencies, event flows, and resource usage are visible and governed holistically. Cost, performance, and compliance are derived from the same service metadata. Architectural decisions can be evolved centrally without touching every consumer.
Releases are lifecycle events. Catalogue, policy, audit, observability — automatic. Releases are lifecycle events the platform understands end-to-end. A release updates the catalogue, validates policy, records audit evidence, and feeds into observability — whether it's a backend deploy, a web build, or a mobile submission. The full chain from commit to production is a single coherent flow.
Single estate view. Cross-cutting queries answerable and actionable. A single coherent view of the entire estate. Cross-cutting queries ("which services depend on this library, haven't been released in 90 days, and are non-compliant?") are answerable and actionable. The catalogue drives governance, not just discovery.
Platform takes corrective action. Drift remediated. Maintenance invisible. The platform takes corrective action based on what it observes. Drift from baselines is remediated, not just flagged. Routine maintenance is invisible. Developers are only pulled in for decisions that require context the platform doesn't have.
Platform-assisted. Correlates cause. Auto-remediates known patterns. Incident response is platform-assisted. The platform correlates releases, alerts, and dependencies to surface probable cause. Known failure patterns trigger automated remediation — restarts, rollbacks, failovers — without waiting for a human.
Fleet-wide changes are first-class. Impact assessed. Policy via metadata. Fleet-wide changes are first-class operations. The platform assesses impact, stages rollouts, and tracks progress across the estate. Policy changes propagate through metadata, not manual updates to individual services.
Level 5 Adaptive Proactive, autonomous where safe
Features assessed against the estate for reuse, complexity, and dependencies. The platform connects ideation to implementation end-to-end. A proposed feature can be assessed against existing services, components, and capabilities to surface reuse opportunities, estimate complexity, and identify dependencies before development begins.
Platform recommends and implements changes based on history and risk. The platform recommends and implements architectural changes based on the estate's history — what's worked, what's failed, what's expensive, and what's secure. Cost optimisation and right-sizing are proactive.
Pipelines adapt to context and risk. Strategies informed by historical patterns. Pipelines adapt to context — deeper validation for high-risk changes, fast-tracking low-risk ones, flagging anomalies in build or test behaviour before they reach production. Release strategies are informed by historical patterns across the estate.
Natural-language querying. Anomalies and insights surfaced automatically. Natural-language and conversational interfaces let developers query the estate. The platform detects anomalies and surfaces insights — including cost trends and emerging risks — without being asked.
Pre-emptive action. Predictive scaling, waste reclamation, auto-patching. The platform acts pre-emptively — scaling ahead of predicted demand, reclaiming unused resources, restarting failing components, and applying patches within policy. Human intervention is reserved for decisions that require judgement.
Self-healing. Root cause from history. Repeated incidents prevented. The platform self-heals where it can, correlates incidents across services, and suggests root cause based on historical patterns. Repeated incidents feed back into the platform to prevent recurrence. Novel issues surface with full context for human diagnosis.
Validated against dependency graph. Auto-staged with human gates. Fleet-wide changes are validated against the dependency graph and risk profile before execution. The platform stages, monitors, and rolls back automatically. Human approval gates protect high-risk actions.

Future Considerations

This model describes platform maturity as it's understood today. The relationship between platforms and AI-driven development is evolving fast enough that what "good" looks like — particularly at the higher levels — will likely shift significantly. Some assumptions worth revisiting:

Developers as primary users

Every level is framed around developer experience. But if AI agents can generate, deploy, and manage services, the platform's most important consumer may not be a human. The human role likely shifts towards architecture and intent — defining what should exist, how it should behave, and what constraints apply — rather than building it directly. The platform becomes the enforcement layer between human intent and AI execution.

Services as the unit of software

The model assumes services are the thing you build, ship, run, and fix. But if generating a service is cheap, the unit may shift. You describe a business capability; the platform decides whether that's a new service, a modification, or a composition of what already exists. Decomposition itself becomes a platform decision rather than a human architectural one.

The lifecycle as distinct phases

Ideation through to Running implies stages with meaningful gaps between them. If the distance from "I want X" to "X is running in production" collapses to minutes, the stages don't disappear — but they stop being phases humans move through. They become internal platform concerns, like compiler phases a developer never thinks about.

Fixing assumes human reasoning

If the platform can understand, modify, and redeploy its own systems, the question shifts from "how do we help developers fix things faster" to "what shrinking class of problems still requires a human?" The interesting boundary is no longer speed of diagnosis but the limits of autonomous remediation.

Migration assumes evolution is hard

If generating a replacement is cheap, "changing" might mean "generate a new version from the current spec plus new requirements, validate against existing behaviour, and cut over" — not carefully evolving the running thing in place. Lifecycle becomes less about managing change and more about managing continuity.

Platform as governance, not automation

The framing today is "maximise time on product work." But if AI handles most infrastructure work and increasingly contributes to product work, the platform's purpose may shift from protecting developer time to being the system of record that makes AI-generated software trustworthy, governable, and auditable — the organisation's memory and judgement.
